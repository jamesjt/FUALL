CIVIL DISCOURSE




"The liberal arts are those subjects or skills that in classical antiquity were considered essential for a free person (liberalis, 'worthy of a free person') to know in order to take an active part in civic life, something that (for ancient Greece) included participating in public debate, defending oneself in court, serving on juries, and most importantly, military service."
— Ernst Robert Curtius, European Literature and the Latin Middle Ages


The primary tool of those arts was — should still be — civil discourse. But what exactly do we mean by the term?




Defining: Discourse


Discourse derives from the Latin discursus, itself from discurrere — dis- ("apart, in different directions") and currere ("to run"). In classical Latin, discursus was physical: the action of running to and fro, scattering in different directions. [1]


In Late Latin, roughly the third through fifth centuries, the meaning shifted from physical movement to verbal exchange — the running about of words between people. This is where discourse first becomes a communicative act. [1]


The crucial turn came in Medieval Latin, within the scholastic philosophy of the sixth through thirteenth centuries. Discursus became the term for the distinctly human mode of reasoning: the mind moving step by step from one known thing to another, from premises to conclusions. [1]


Thomas Aquinas drew an explicit contrast between discursive reasoning — human, sequential, effortful — and intuitive understanding, which he attributed to the divine as immediate and whole. For Aquinas, human knowledge "forms a sure judgment about a truth by the discursive process of his reason." [2] The word carried a sense of intellectual labor: the mind running between ideas to connect them.


When the word entered Middle English around 1325, borrowed through the Middle French discours, its earliest recorded meaning was not "conversation" but "process of understanding, reasoning, thought." This is the sense Shakespeare draws on when Hamlet says "a beast that wants discourse of reason" [3] — an animal that lacks the capacity for reasoned thought, not one that lacks conversation. Only in the 1550s and 1580s did "discourse" shift to mean communication of thought in words and then formal treatment of a subject in speech or writing. [1]


Today the word has diluted further. "Discourse" is applied to everything from academic treatises to social media arguments. The internet era has even produced "the Discourse" as a proper noun — meaning ongoing, heated, often unproductive public argument — which is essentially a degradation of the term into something closer to its opposite.


By discourse I mean something that recovers the word's deeper history: the running about of ideas within conversation, specifically in search of truth, understanding, and consensus. This meaning holds together both the Late Latin sense of exchange between people and the Medieval Latin sense of reason moving between ideas. Discourse is not mere talk. It is the effortful, sequential, back-and-forth labor of human reasoning conducted between minds.




Defining: Civil


Civil comes from the Latin civilis, "relating to a citizen, pertaining to public life, befitting a citizen." [4] Even in Latin, the word extended by association to "popular, affable, courteous" — as though the Romans assumed that civic participation and agreeable conduct naturally went together. The root is civis, "citizen," connected to civitas — not merely a political unit but the community of citizens that constitutes a city-state. The deeper Proto-Indo-European root *kei- carries the sense of lying down, settling, making a home — with a secondary meaning of "beloved, dear." [4] Civitas was a community of people who had settled together and chosen to share a common life.


The word entered English in the late fourteenth century through Old French, initially meaning "relating to civil law or life; pertaining to the internal affairs of a state." [4] By the 1550s it had acquired the sense "not barbarous, civilized." The purely social meaning — civil as mere politeness, the avoidance of rudeness — developed later still and gradually became dominant. Merriam-Webster's usage note captures the degradation: "civil often suggests little more than the avoidance of overt rudeness." [5] That is a long fall from conduct befitting a citizen.


Civility, in its original force, is the disposition required to sustain shared life — not a social nicety but the conduct worthy of citizens who have chosen to settle together and govern in common.




Civil Discourse


Civil discourse, then, is not polite conversation. It is the exchange of ideas as citizens — the activity that free people engage in to govern shared life together. It is discussion intended to enhance understanding and, where possible, to persuade. Its topics can span from how everything in the cosmos relates to each other to how to properly use a single term.


While many institutions claim to foster civil discourse, few seem to actually deliver it. Successful examination of deep topics is likely to be long-lasting, multi-dimensional, and may require multi-medium discussion. Significant progress can be made quickly through in-person exchange, but writing allows evidence to be linked and arguments to be restated, clarified, and amended outside the heat of the moment. When institutions skip the difficult, painstaking work of establishing shared meaning and instead offer performance — what I've elsewhere called Sneaky Candy — it may look like discourse, but it fails to deliver the substance that makes discourse worthwhile.

CIVILITY


"I have endured what no living mortal on this earth has borne — I've lifted up to my own lips and kissed the hands of the man who killed my son."
— Priam, The Trojan King, to Achilles. Iliad XXIV (trans. Ian Johnston)


Priam does not suppress his anguish. He carries it into the tent of his enemy and, through it, achieves what force could not. This moment amid the Trojan War is evidence that even amid grief and rage, we can reach toward something higher — not by numbing our intensity but by directing it toward understanding.


Civility in this context means that positions are argued genuinely, that benefit of the doubt is given toward the intentions of other parties, and that participants hold a willingness to change their own beliefs while trying to change the beliefs of others. This exchange can be hot, intense, and emotional. Being overly sensitive to that heat does just as much harm to civility as being aggressive. Priam wept before Achilles; Achilles wept with him. The exchange was no less civil for its intensity.


Civility is the spirit that guides us upward — away from what is animal within us and closer to what is truly human. We named our own species Homo sapiens — the wise ones. Our capacity for understanding, for reasoning beyond instinct toward wisdom, is our defining trait. Civility is what allows that capacity to function between people, turning rage and pain into respect and understanding, even toward our enemies in times of conflict and tragedy.




THREE DIMENSIONS OF DISCOURSE


Discourse moves along three interrelated dimensions: Deliberation, Dialogue, and Debate. Each is rooted in the classical trivium — Grammar, Logic, and Rhetoric — and each is tied more closely to one of those foundations than the others.


A productive discourse will generally transition fluidly or "run about" these dimensions. Complex topics demand the iterative clarification of deliberation, the connective understanding of dialogue, and the adjudication of competing claims in debate. The structure I offer here is not a rigid sequence but a way of recognizing how discourse flows and where it might break down.




THE CLASSICAL TRIVIUM


Before exploring the three dimensions, it helps to clarify the three arts that undergird them.


Grammar, in this context, does not mean the basic structures and symbols of language. It means clarity of message and intent — the domain of semantics and pragmatics rather than phonetics or syntax. Two skills matter especially: the ability to attach precise meaning to words (semantics) and the ability to understand what another participant actually means (pragmatic competence).


Logic is the process of understanding the relationships among facts and ideas — the means of reasoning, classification, inference, and emergence. It is how we determine what is true, or how confident we can be that something is true.


Rhetoric is efficiency in grammar and logic for the purpose of articulating ideas clearly — a largely Quintilian understanding of the term. I do not mean the assessment of any effective means of persuasion, as not all means of persuasion are civil. Persuasion through pathos alone is actively unreasonable. The efficiency of rhetoric is predicated on the consensus and understanding established through grammar and logic.




DELIBERATION


Grammar guided by Logic.


Deliberation is concerned with the establishment of facts and definitions. It is often slow and painstaking, subject to digressions, yet indispensable for grounding any subsequent exchange.


This dimension strives to build a foundation of definitions, facts, evidence, and the strength of confidence in evidence toward a claim. Even between two good-faith participants, a shaky foundation can prevent meaningful resolution.


Clarifying the bounds of disagreement as one on facts can be just as important as any other resolution.


Today, semantics is often dismissed as trivial, or when conflict arises in its domain, it is treated as an obstacle too annoying to overcome. This dismissal is one of our culture's most significant failures in creating civil discourse.


Taking the time to properly define terms can be tedious but it is a common and serious pitfall which we can see in lots of conversations pretending to be debate [the Munk Debate on Political Correctness and the IQ2 debate on Safe Spaces]. So often we see people attaching even slightly different concepts to a term, which can entirely ruin any conversation by having participants talk past each other — causing frustration, confusion, and anger that eventually kills the civility and the desire to continue.


On the other side sits pragmatic competence — the ability to understand another participant's intended meaning. Semantics is nuanced and time-consuming, and it is often worth debating. But progress can frequently be made through a temporary agreement to attach a term to a concept and move forward, trusting that participants are operating in good faith about what they mean.


Civility in deliberation: When facts require significant expertise, or when terminology is still under debate but isn't the core issue, tentatively granting premises for the sake of progress is generally civil. The clarity and impact of an idea can sometimes be easier to grasp in dialogue, so the dimensions often work together even here.


Tools:
— Hopping into dialogue: understanding the relationship of facts is often needed to reach consensus on those facts themselves, even before a full dialogic exchange.
— Citing sources to ground contested claims.


Pitfalls:
— Failing to define important terms.
— Discourse collapsing into a lecture when one side has significantly more knowledge of the topic.
— Inability to create consensus resulting in a loop: "no, it isn't" / "yes, it is." When this happens, hop into dialogue: why isn't it? Why is it?




DIALOGUE


Logic guided by Grammar — much of which is tentative or exploratory, as it tries to articulate the relationship of things.


Dialogue cultivates understanding. It connects and synthesizes facts and ideas — both what others are saying and how facts relate to one another — seeking clarity and coherence. It uses various logical tools to explore how the facts and definitions established within deliberation tie into the core propositions of the discourse itself.


Often this looks like participants taking turns thinking through these relationships, checking for agreement or surfacing disagreements. Dialogue will frequently loop back into deliberation as new terms and claims emerge. At this stage, discourse remains primarily concerned with establishing truths and confidence levels. It is especially important during dialogue that the listener focus on denotation, giving almost no regard to connotation.


One of the distinctions to be drawn between a lecture and discourse is how participants in dialogue are often expected to hold two different understandings or come from two different areas of knowledge, each trying to see what can be gained from the other. Gray or fuzzy areas of knowledge and logic shrink over time as we gather new or better data and improve our methodology.


Resolutions in dialogue:
— Resolution of ignorance: Both parties recognize that not enough is known about the facts or their relationships to move toward weighing values and actions.
— Resolution of a new path: Both parties realize that their prior beliefs don't align with what is discovered through dialogue, and both adopt a new position.


Civility in dialogue: Recognition of authority is important in discourse on deep topics. Experts may hold different understandings of facts and their relationships. Giving experts the benefit of the doubt until there is reason not to is part of civility — though this does not mean their claims should go unexplained, unchallenged, or unchecked.


It is also recognition that dialogue often runs into the boundary where what we know meets what we do not, and how we gain confidence in our understanding of how to move forward despite unknowns.


Aristotle distinguished between episteme — systematic knowledge that can be demonstrated — and techne, the knowledge of how to make or do, which involves judgment in the face of particulars that cannot be fully systematized. There are true technicians of every field, those who have studied a domain so deeply that their understanding allows them to develop strong intuition into the unknown.


Consider Barbara McClintock, who identified genetic transposition in maize in the 1940s. Her conclusions ran ahead of what the available methods could formally demonstrate, and the scientific establishment dismissed her work for decades. Molecular biology eventually vindicated her with a Nobel Prize in 1983 — but for forty years, her intuition was better than the field's formal knowledge. Or Alfred Wegener, who proposed continental drift in 1912 based on geological and paleontological pattern recognition. Geologists rejected it for fifty years because he could not provide a mechanism. Plate tectonics proved him right in the 1960s.


Recall the distinction Aquinas drew [2]: discursive reasoning is the step-by-step labor of moving from premise to conclusion, while intuitive understanding grasps truth immediately and whole. Discourse is the tool for most of us, most of the time. But those who have spent years within a domain can develop a kind of sight — similar to perceiving illusory contours connecting things — where their intuition runs ahead of what can yet be formally proven.


Yet a credential does not make an expert, and few experts develop the depth of intuition that makes them truly reliable technicians of the unknown. Even still we must rely on these technicians, else the speed of progress comes to a crawl. They are always at the edge, pushing understanding forward with a more accurate sense of what lies ahead than others possess. There is a great deal of unknown and gray in any field, and recognizing this is part of intellectual honesty.


Tools:
— Restating the other participant's premises to confirm understanding.
— Assuming a statement is subtle until shown otherwise.
— Assuming a counterintuitive claim is intentional until shown otherwise.
— Reasonable benefit of the doubt in authority.
— Analogy and metaphor.
— Engaging with the understanding that you may be wrong — and that a mistake by your opponent is not your victory.


Pitfalls:
— Logical fallacies — mistakes in understanding relationships between facts or ideas.
— One side pushing for a resolution of ignorance as a way of dismissing authority or references they distrust, whether that distrust is valid or not.




DEBATE


Logic guided by Rhetoric — efficient articulation of ideas becomes important in reaching a clear resolution.


Debate aims at forging consensus toward paths in navigating the unknown to form truer beliefs. It is where we weigh competing claims against values, risks, and costs, refining what we believe through rational prioritization. Done well, debate brings collective understanding closer to truth and enables us to justify beliefs as knowledge.


By this stage, language should have been refined and made efficient through the prior dimensions, allowing for eloquent articulation — by which I mean language that efficiently carries the denotation of ideas with as little misinterpretation as possible. I do not mean poetic. I do not mean rhythmic preaching.


Resolutions in debate:
— Resolution of boundaries: When persuasion is not achievable, we strive to better understand the bounds of disagreement and what changes in circumstances or knowledge could create agreement.
— Resolution of persuasion: Agreement is reached.
— Resolution of conditional persuasion: Persuasion predicated on statements from authority or on claims we have some confidence in but are not yet certain of. Someone can be convinced that something is more likely without being convinced it is likely.
— Resolution of incommensurability: The participants discover they cannot agree on foundational facts or definitions sufficient to proceed. The discourse ends not in agreement or persuasion but in a clear articulation of where and why agreement is impossible. This is distinct from a resolution of boundaries — it is the recognition that the ground itself cannot be shared, and that no amount of further discourse will change that without new evidence or experience outside the conversation.


Civility in debate: Changing our beliefs is difficult and can lead to intense feelings. Intensity that surfaces as anger or frustration — even when the exchange is otherwise civil — is often directed at the self, at past teachers, or at the surprise of encountering genuinely new information. When discourse flows back into dialogue or deliberation, that lingering intensity can be harmful. Taking a beat to allow participants to collect themselves is often necessary for moving forward.


Tools:
— Steel-manning: assume the opponent is making the best version of their argument until you know they aren't. The progress of ideas deserves better than preying on poor mistakes in rhetoric.
— A break for reflection and analysis, especially when transitioning between dialogue and debate.
— Providing reference or summary of the deliberation and dialogue for any audience whose pragmatic competence may suffer from those exchanges being "off screen" or tediously long.


Pitfalls:
— Attempts at persuasion through pathos.
— Relying on ethos when logos can be reached.
— Straw-manning — the fallacy most likely to arise in debate, as increasingly efficient language allows for easier misinterpretation.
— Refinement of language will come with some cost in clarity for people who did not participate in or observe the earlier dimensions.
