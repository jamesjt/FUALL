- **Progressivism can be distilled to a single animating idea: reasoned inquiry unlocks pathways to a be**
  tter life, and a better life empowers greater inquiry. This philosophy rests on three interconnected propositions.1. Knowledge is possible and instrumental.
  Observing, experimenting, and theorizing enable us to map the causal structure of our world. Such understanding is not merely academic; it furnishes levers for real‑world improvement.
  2. Well‑being is desirable and improvable.
  Progressivism treats human flourishing—longevity, liberty from pain, opportunity for meaningful activity—as an ethical imperative. Suffering caused by ignorance or remediable conditions is viewed as a problem to solve, not a fate to endure. Because reason reveals the avoidable sources of hardship, moral duty converges with empirical possibility: we should act precisely because we can act effectively.
  3. Improvement and inquiry reinforce each other.
  When interventions succeed—curing disease, boosting literacy, reducing drudgery—society has a growing amount of resources that can be allocated for things other than merely survival. Those freed resources can be redirected to research, education, and exploration, accelerating the pace of discovery. Each scientific or technological breakthrough, in turn, opens new avenues for enhancing well‑being. The loop is self‑amplifying: knowledge begets well‑being; well‑being begets further knowledge.
  Seen over long horizons, this virtuous cycle sketches an asymptotic approach to what earlier cultures might have called a golden age or era of enlightenment—a state where pervasive understanding curbs material scarcity and mitigates avoidable suffering. The vision is not utopian in the sense of perfection; rather, it is progressive in the literal sense of continual movement toward higher peaks of both comprehension and quality of life.
  > **Extended notes:**
  > Critics warn of overconfidence in reason, noting unintended consequences and ethical dilemmas that emerge from rapid change. Progressivism answers by emphasizing reflexivity: new knowledge must include the study of its own side‑effects, feeding them back into the next iteration of inquiry. In this way, the same epistemic humility that drives rigorous science also guards against hubris.
  > In sum, progressivism—understood as the reciprocal investment in understanding reality and uplifting humanity—posits a positive feedback loop capable of propelling civilization toward ever‑greater enlightenment. Its promise lies not in any single institution or doctrine, but in the enduring synergy between curious minds and compassionate aims.

 because it feels intuitive, but the**
  reality is far more complex. The forces we call “left” and “right” form a multi-dimensional political manifold—a system in which many interconnected variables push and pull on one another.
  My perspective comes from balancing strategy games. In a well-tuned game, players choose among several viable paths to victory. Altering the power of one unit never affects that unit alone; it shifts the relative value of every other unit and, if mishandled, can create a dominant “meta” that crowds out alternative strategies. Good designers therefore adjust multiple variables in concert to keep the overall system healthy.
  Politics works the same way. Consider minimum wage. Milton Friedman did not argue against it in isolation; he paired its removal with a negative income tax (effectively a universal basic income) to ensure a floor of well-being while freeing wages to float. Lower labor costs could expand employment, but only if a separate mechanism guarantees that basic needs are met. Like a game designer, the policymaker must tune several knobs at once to preserve balance—job availability, purchasing power, and price levels—rather than yanking a single lever and hoping for the best.
  When we collapse this intricate system into a single left-right axis, we compress countless dimensions into one variable: the appetite for change. “Progressive” means the status quo is inadequate and bold reforms are worth the risk; “Conservative” means the present order is valuable and should be altered only with caution. Yet even this first-order spectrum hides deeper layers. Each policy area—justice, economic opportunity, cultural norms—has its own sub-spectra of costs, risks, and trade-offs.

- **On the cohesiveness of Left-leaning values—progressivism, liberalism, and collectivism

One persiste**
  nt obstacle to progress is cost: opportunity cost, displacement cost, and exploration cost. The link between progressivism and collectivism lies largely in how society handles those displacement and exploration costs.
  - Exploration costs. Ambitious projects such as the International Space Station, LIGO, and other large-scale scientific endeavors require resources far beyond what private benefactors can reliably supply. Because we all pay taxes, we can pool those resources and fund reality-exploring efforts—conceptually and literally—rather than relying on a handful of ultra-rich patrons to push science forward. Yet federal research budgets are being cut even as the need for collective investment grows.
  - Displacement costs. Progress often displaces workers. Consider driverless cars and trucks: automation would be a huge boon for society, but its cost will fall on truckers who lose their livelihoods. Collectively, we should shoulder those costs rather than leaving displaced workers behind.
  - Opportunity costs and liberalism. We are notoriously bad at predicting who will generate the next breakthrough—sorting for potential, wisdom, or good ideas at scale is extremely difficult and heavily influenced by luck. Expanding individual liberties gives more people room to explore, experiment, and innovate. Thus, liberalism and progressivism reinforce each other: the greater the liberty individuals possess, the more opportunities exist for genuine progress.
  In short, progressivism urges us to move society forward, collectivism asks us to share the exploration and displacement costs that progress entails, and liberalism expands the pool of potential innovators whose lucky insights may drive the next big leap.

- **Terms for political movements
We need a more sophisticated way to discuss politics. A political move**
  ment is a set of issues behind which people concentrate power, so its name must clearly and intuitively signal what it seeks to change, conserve, or achieve.
  A movement should at least identify a problem, the change it hopes to enact, or the element it wants to preserve—and, ideally, outline a plan for doing so. Sometimes that plan is missing because no one has yet investigated the root of the problem; the first step, then, is to secure resources to study the issue and design the most effective strategy. In worse circumstances, the harm is already great—often small harms at vast scale that “make us all dumber.” How long do we wait for perfect knowledge before taking our first stabs at becoming less dumb?
  Every movement must define its goals, name itself accordingly, and select (or anoint) principal leaders. Those leaders might found the movement or arise later, but the brand-bearers must embody its values. Members must hold leaders accountable to those principles, recognizing that both values and strategy can evolve and that leaders must communicate such changes.
  When followers identify with a group yet do not understand—or practice—the movement’s values, allegiance drifts from principle to personality. The result is no longer a political movement but a political cult: people follow power irrationally. Members must retain the power to remove, challenge, or criticize leaders who stray from the movement’s purpose, even though this can create endless factional splits. This is one of the hardest tasks—but we must manage it, or we’re just fucked.
  In summary:
  Labeling a cause merely “Left” or “Right” is useless for defining a movement, but discussing the underlying values is not.
  Dig deeper: articulate a value structure, a goal, and (preferably) a strategy; appoint leaders committed to those values; and replace them if they betray the mission—even if doing so seems to weaken the movement in the short run. Better to correct a mistake than let an unprincipled leader deform the cause.
  We saw this danger with BLM, with portions of the progressive movement, and with whatever “Left” and “Right” now mean.
  Donald Trump at least modeled one constructive step: MAGA (and later MAHA) framed a problem and coined a label. The next step would be to spell out detailed value structures and metrics so we can measure efficacy, hold leaders to principle, and debate whether a given strategy is working.
  That is where we must go in understanding—and renovating—the Left-Right conversation, rather than discarding the idea because it feels inadequate today.
