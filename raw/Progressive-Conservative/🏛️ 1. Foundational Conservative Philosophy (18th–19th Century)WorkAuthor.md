# 🏛️ 1. Foundational Conservative Philosophy (18th–19th Century)WorkAuthor

🏛️ 1. Foundational Conservative Philosophy (18th–19th Century)WorkAuthor / YearSignificanceReflections on the Revolution in FranceEdmund Burke (1790)Foundational conservative text. Burke critiques the French Revolution for dismantling inherited institutions too quickly. He defends tradition, gradual change, and the intergenerational social contract.Essay on the Generative Principle of Political ConstitutionsJoseph de Maistre (1810)A Catholic counter-Enlightenment thinker who defends monarchy and divine order; emphasizes authority, not reason, as the foundation of society.On Liberty (ironically)John Stuart Mill (1859)Though usually claimed by liberals, Mill’s warnings against tyranny of the majority and defense of individual conscience have deeply influenced conservative libertarians.Democracy in AmericaAlexis de Tocqueville (1835–40)While not a self-described conservative, Tocqueville warned of soft despotism and the erosion of intermediary institutions like religion and local associations.

🧱 2. Classical and Traditional Conservatism (19th–early 20th)WorkAuthor / YearContributionConservatismPeter Viereck (1956)One of the first attempts to define U.S. conservatism as a philosophical tradition, not just anti-liberalism. Defends Burkean moderation.The Conservative MindRussell Kirk (1953)Seminal U.S. postwar text outlining 6 “canons” of conservative thought—e.g., belief in a transcendent order, tradition, prudence, and prescription. He frames Burke as the archetypal conservative.OrthodoxyG.K. Chesterton (1908)A poetic, theological defense of tradition and “common sense” as bulwarks against modern relativism and social engineering.

🗽 3. Modern Conservatism and Post-War Realignments (1940s–1980s)WorkAuthor / YearKey IdeasThe Road to SerfdomFriedrich Hayek (1944)Austrian economist warning that planning leads to tyranny. Foundations of libertarian conservatism. Not a cultural conservative, but deeply influential on Reagan/Thatcher thought.Ideas Have ConsequencesRichard Weaver (1948)Southern conservative warning against nominalism and relativism. Argues cultural decay follows from metaphysical denial of truth.God and Man at YaleWilliam F. Buckley Jr. (1951)Launches the National Review brand of anti-communist, religious, and free-market conservatism. Marks start of modern U.S. conservative movement.The Conservative AffirmationWillmoore Kendall (1963)Key in fusing populism and tradition. Stresses majoritarian values, cultural continuity, and skepticism of abstract rights.WitnessWhittaker Chambers (1952)Memoir of a former Communist turned staunch conservative. Highly influential in forming Cold War anti-communist ethos.

🧠 4. Recent Theoretical & Global Conservatism (1990s–2020s)WorkAuthor / YearModern RelevanceWhy Liberalism FailedPatrick Deneen (2018)Argues both left and right liberalism hollow out tradition and community. Rooted in postliberal critique. Popular among traditionalist conservatives.After VirtueAlasdair MacIntyre (1981)Neo-Aristotelian critique of Enlightenment moral discourse. Often cited by communitarian and religious conservatives.The Demon in DemocracyRyszard Legutko (2016)Polish philosopher argues Western democracies are becoming ideologically intolerant. Offers a conservative critique of liberal homogeneity.The Reactionary MindCorey Robin (2011; rev. 2018)A critical—but widely referenced—book arguing conservatism is fundamentally about defending hierarchy. Not a conservative work, but central to debates.
