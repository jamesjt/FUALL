# On Progressivism

Progress, at its core, stems from the belief that what we have in the present is not sufficient, and that we should continuously explore ways to improve. However, the notion of “better” is central to the friction between progressivism and conservatism—defining what is “better,” and how to achieve it, can cause major disagreements.

Goals and AlignmentAt the highest level, progress requires a shared goal. We must determine:
- What are we trying to make better?
- What is lacking in the current situation?
- Inventing or adopting new technology.
- Changing the organizational structure, laws, and rules.
- Shifting cultural attitudes that may have become complacent.
- Improving leadership to ensure effective agreement on goals and strategies.

The Cost of ProgressOne of the key challenges is that progress often demands dismantling existing structures—a process that can be costly and disruptive. In some cases, you can build on what already works. In other cases, “tearing it down” may be the only way to fix deep-rooted flaws. Yet this teardown carries its own risks:
- Systemic Impact: Removing one element can have far-reaching effects on related systems.
- Dependencies and Synergies: You have to identify and protect the “baby” before throwing out the “bathwater,” or risk losing valuable aspects altogether.
- Burn It Down?: Sometimes the system is so broken or corrupt that it must be scrapped entirely, which is what some believe is happening with US Aid or the Department of Education.
Progress also has other costs:
- Opportunity Cost: Resources directed toward one area of improvement cannot go toward maintaining existing systems or addressing other needs.
- Impact on Individuals: For example, driverless cars might revolutionize transportation but come at the expense of truckers, taxi drivers, and others whose livelihoods depend on driving.

Fracturing and DebateProgressivism naturally fractures along several lines:
- Which issues merit immediate focus? Many might agree education is ineffective, but some feel healthcare is a more pressing concern.
- How should we solve a given problem? Even if everyone agrees education needs reform, there can be profound disagreements on the strategy, tactics, and practical execution.
- Mixed Views: People can be progressive on one issue (e.g., education) and conservative on another (e.g., healthcare).
- Skepticism and Risk Aversion: Without a clear path to improvement, some are reluctant to change a flawed system, fearing that attempts to fix it might make it worse.
Because of these factors, rallying a unified progressive movement is difficult. A single shared goal (like “improve education”) often shatters into disagreements over cost, priorities, and methods.

Relationship to Foundational IdeologyPolitical or power structures reflect cultural values. A collective-leaning culture might agree that workers displaced by driverless technology should receive substantial public support. Others might insist that individuals, not the collective, must bear those costs.
Similarly, when to pay the cost of progress—now or in the future—varies by ideology. Some prefer to “mortgage the future” to avoid immediate disruption. Others argue the future is sacred, so sacrifices should be made now to ensure greater benefit for generations to come.

ConclusionProgressivism, like any system of thought about power and governance, is deeply interwoven with cultural beliefs about what “better” means and who should bear the costs of getting there. We need more precise language to communicate:
- Where progress is needed
- What the specific goals are
- How those goals should be achieved
- Who pays the costs, and when

Ultimately, progress is about building a future we deem superior to the present—yet defining and funding that future is a complex process, inevitably rife with disagreements, trade-offs, and the ever-present risk of losing what still works in pursuit of what might be better.
