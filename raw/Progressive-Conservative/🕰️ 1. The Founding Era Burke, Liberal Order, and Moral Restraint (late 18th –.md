# 🕰️ 1. The Founding Era Burke, Liberal Order, and Moral Restraint (late 18th –

🕰️ 1. The Founding Era: Burke, Liberal Order, and Moral Restraint (late 18th – mid 19th c.)- Edmund Burke (1790) lays the groundwork for both camps. He defends liberty and markets within a framework of tradition, religion, and inherited institutions.
- Conservatism becomes the art of preserving liberty through restraint—a hybrid of liberal procedure and moral-cultural inheritance.
- Alexis de Tocqueville (1830s) adds a warning: democracy without virtue collapses into “soft despotism.”
→ This synthesis—liberty with virtue—is the seedbed for both liberal and postliberal conservatism.

🏛️ 2. Classical Liberal Dominance & Early Fractures (late 19th – mid 20th c.)- Industrial capitalism, Enlightenment rationalism, and legalistic liberalism expand across Europe and America.
- Conservatives like John Stuart Mill and later Hayek argue liberty must be defended against centralized moral or economic planning.
- Burkean caution remains, but liberty becomes the core value of what we later call liberal conservatism.
- Catholic intellectuals (de Maistre, later Maritain) critique this from the sidelines, insisting liberalism corrodes virtue and community.

🗽 3. Fusionism: The Cold War Coalition (1945–1980s)- U.S. conservatism merges:
- Libertarianism (Hayek, Friedman) → economic liberty
- Traditionalism (Kirk, Weaver) → moral order
- Anti-Communism (Buckley, Chambers) → defense of the West
- This “fusionism” is brokered by William F. Buckley Jr. and institutions like National Review.
- Liberal conservatism dominates.
- It champions free markets, limited government, and cultural continuity without enforcing doctrine.
- Key Tension Emerges:
- Moral traditionalists begin asking: Can free markets sustain a moral culture—or do they dissolve it?
→ Fusionism papers over this tension, but does not resolve it.

🧨 4. Crisis Points: The Post–Cold War Void (1990s–2000s)- The collapse of communism removes the unifying enemy.
- Neoconservatism rises—supporting liberal democracy promotion abroad and market globalism at home.
- Social conservatives lose ground on abortion, family policy, and sexual norms.
- Patrick Deneen, Alasdair MacIntyre, and Rod Dreher begin developing critiques of liberal modernity:
- Liberalism lacks a vision of the good.
- Autonomy is hollow without moral formation.
- Markets and rights discourse erode community.
→ This is the intellectual ferment that will power postliberalism.

⚡ 5. Breakaway: The Rise of Postliberal Conservatism (2010s–2020s)- 2016: Donald Trump upends the liberal conservative order. While not ideologically coherent, he:
- Attacks globalism
- Challenges libertarian orthodoxy
- Uses state power assertively
→ For many, Trump is the cultural rupture that cracks open the space postliberals now fill.
- Patrick Deneen's Why Liberalism Failed (2018) becomes a touchstone:
- Liberalism succeeded too well—it liberated us from everything meaningful.
- Adrian Vermeule proposes “common good constitutionalism” (2020)
- The state must actively shape public morality.
- Neutrality is a fiction.
- The law should promote substantive human flourishing.
- Sohrab Ahmari, Ryszard Legutko, and others amplify the cause in media and think tanks.
