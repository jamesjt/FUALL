# From Guardians of Liberty to Architects of the Good The Rise of Postliberal

From Guardians of Liberty to Architects of the Good: The Rise of Postliberal Conservatism

For much of the modern era, conservatism in the West has been defined by its role as a custodian of the liberal order. It sought to preserve the rule of law, private property, limited government, and individual liberty—not because these were revolutionary ideals, but because they offered stability amid ideological storms. This fusion of classical liberalism and moral traditionalism came to be known as liberal conservatism. It married Edmund Burke’s reverence for tradition with Friedrich Hayek’s warning against central planning, defending freedom through restraint and tradition through freedom.

But in the 21st century, that synthesis is unraveling. A growing chorus of thinkers argues that liberalism itself—not just its progressive form—has corroded the moral, cultural, and communal foundations that conservatives once hoped to preserve. These critics are part of an emerging intellectual tendency known as postliberal conservatism. Where liberal conservatives sought to defend liberty under ordered institutions, postliberals believe that liberty without a shared vision of the good is not self-sustaining—it is self-destructive.

The Fusionist Age: Liberty and Order
The foundations of liberal conservatism were laid in the late 18th century with Edmund Burke’s Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790), which warned that societies cannot be rationally reconstructed from scratch. Burke championed incremental reform grounded in inherited norms. Later thinkers like Tocqueville added warnings about how democracy, untethered from religion and civil society, could descend into tyranny by mass conformity.

In the 20th century, this blend of caution and liberty crystallized into fusionism, a Cold War alliance between traditionalists, libertarians, and anti-communists. William F. Buckley Jr., through National Review, brokered the marriage: moral order would be maintained by churches and communities, while the state would remain neutral and limited. The free market was cast as both morally sound and socially stabilizing.

For decades, this formula defined the mainstream right in the U.S. and parts of Europe. Liberal conservatism seemed triumphant, especially after the fall of the Soviet Union. But beneath its success, contradictions smoldered.

The Seeds of Rebellion
By the 1990s and 2000s, the right’s libertarian wing had become dominant in policy, while its traditionalist wing was losing ground in culture. Global markets expanded, but family structures frayed. The sexual revolution continued, but churches emptied. Conservatives could win elections while still watching the moral ecology they valued disintegrate.

A group of thinkers began to ask: What if liberal institutions themselves—markets, rights discourse, procedural neutrality—are the problem? Philosophers like Alasdair MacIntyre (After Virtue, 1981) and later Patrick Deneen (Why Liberalism Failed, 2018) argued that liberalism doesn’t liberate—it isolates. By reducing society to individuals and the state, it dissolves the intermediary institutions (family, faith, locality) that make liberty meaningful.

Others went further. Harvard law professor Adrian Vermeule called for “common good constitutionalism,” arguing that laws and institutions should not aim to maximize autonomy, but to promote substantive human flourishing. Catholic postliberals like Sohrab Ahmari and Ryszard Legutko began demanding a “politics of the good,” not just a politics of procedure.

Trump and the Break
The 2016 election of Donald Trump—though ideologically inconsistent—was culturally revolutionary. It shattered the Reagan-era consensus, sidelined free-market dogma, and created space for postliberals to step forward. Trump’s aggressive posture on trade, his rhetorical defense of Christianity, and his rejection of globalist liberalism gave symbolic weight to ideas already germinating in intellectual circles.

Postliberal conservatives now argue that liberalism is not failing because it’s been betrayed, but because it’s been fulfilled. The endless expansion of choice and rights leads not to virtue, but to nihilism. Neutral institutions cannot sustain the moral commitments needed for social cohesion. A society that refuses to say what is good, they claim, cannot remain good for long.

The New Divide
Today, the right is fractured. Liberal conservatives—like David French or the editors of National Review—still defend pluralism, free markets, and civil liberties as essential guardrails of any decent society. Postliberals, by contrast, argue that neutrality is a fiction: every regime promotes some moral order. If the current order promotes expressive individualism, then conservatives must offer an alternative—one rooted in faith, nation, family, and the common good.

This debate is no longer academic. It is reshaping political platforms, media ecosystems, and cultural battles from school curricula to economic policy. Whether liberal conservatism can reform itself—or whether postliberalism will supplant it—is now the central ideological struggle on the political right.
