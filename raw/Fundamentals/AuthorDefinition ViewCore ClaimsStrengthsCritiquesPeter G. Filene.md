“An Obituary for ‘The Progressive Movement’” (1970)There is no unified movement. Progressivism is a vague label covering many incompatible agendas.- Progressives had different classes, goals, and regional interests.
- No common ideology, leaders, or consistent objectives.
- “Progressivism” is a post-hoc invention.Forces clarity by rejecting mythologizing.
Encourages disaggregation of reforms.Overcorrects—ignores shared rhetorical styles and moral aims.
Can paralyze synthesis.Daniel T. Rodgers
“In Search of Progressivism” (1982)Progressivism is a constellation of reform energies, not a doctrinal unity.- Identifies 3 major “impulses”: anti-monopoly, social efficiency, and moral uplift.
- Suggests coherence via overlapping moral vocabularies.
- Less a party or program, more a rhetorical and cultural mood.Nuanced; allows flexibility.
Influenced most current scholarship.
Does not require total coherence.Risks vagueness: does anything count as progressivism?
Hard to define policy implications.Michael McGerr
A Fierce Discontent (2003)Progressivism was a coherent middle-class revolution seeking to transform American values.- Reformers shared a radical desire to “remake Americans.”
- United by moralism, not technocracy.
- More sweeping than often assumed—sexuality, leisure, class.Reasserts agency and shared goals.
Makes progressivism feel ideologically alive.May overstate coherence.
Doesn’t fully resolve regional/racial variation.Robert Wiebe
The Search for Order (1967)Progressivism = professional class’s response to industrial disorder.- New “national class” of experts wanted bureaucratic order.
- Focus on system-building, not just justice.
- Reform = reorganization.Illuminates managerial side of progressivism.
Matches rise of professions, schools, policy sciences.Downplays moral or populist elements.
Misses racial and gender dynamics.Steven Parsons et al.
Oxford Research Encyclopedia (2017)Progressivism was diverse but structured. Best understood as a reform era with shared commitments, though not a singular movement.- Period-based definition.
- Brings in newer threads: race, gender, transnationalism.
- Encourages thematic, not monolithic, study.Offers balanced synthesis of 50+ years of debate.
Best for teaching or overview.Doesn’t take a strong theoretical stance.
Descriptive over analytic.

 2. How the debate shaped modern “progressive” politics (21st century)Modern uses of the word progressive—especially in U.S. politics—carry traces of all these historiographical debates:
a. Filene’s fragmentation thesis → reflected in today’s progressive coalition politics- “Progressive” can mean socialists, Green New Deal advocates, anti-racist organizers, or pro-science technocrats—not always ideologically aligned.
- The term unites against status quo or centrist drift, not necessarily for a common vision.
b. Rodgers’ cluster model → maps directly to issue-based progressive activism- Activists often focus on overlapping reform “impulses”:
- Economic justice (Bernie Sanders, labor organizers)
- Racial and gender equity (Movement for Black Lives, intersectional feminism)
- Environmental urgency (Sunrise Movement, degrowth advocates)
- Each speaks in moral-progressive language without needing full programmatic alignment.
c. McGerr’s moral unity thesis → visible in progressive identity politics- Many progressives today are united by a strong desire to remake societal norms—around gender, race, consumption, and identity.
- McGerr’s idea of moral remaking fits progressive emphasis on “anti-oppression” and “cultural decolonization.”
d. Wiebe’s managerial thesis → lives on in liberal technocracy and policy progressivism- Think tanks, data-driven policy, and “evidence-based governance” (e.g., Elizabeth Warren, Vox Media) follow the Progressive Era pattern of “fixing” society through expert design.
e. Parsons’ synthesis → adopted in academic discourse and political branding- Modern scholars and politicians alike avoid rigid definitions.
- “Progressive” becomes a flexible identity label that suggests a reformist orientation, but allows for internal pluralism.

TL;DR:
The 20th-century debate over what Progressivism meant helped shape the 21st-century ambiguity of the word. Today’s “progressives” are both heirs to and hostages of this unresolved legacy—a coalition of reform energies rather than a singular movement.
