# How the Enlightenment seeded progressivismBelow is a step‑by‑step look at how

How the Enlightenment seeded progressivismBelow is a step‑by‑step look at how the 18th‑century Enlightenment turned the idea of progress from a scattered hunch into a dominant historical narrative—and how that narrative later crystallised into the political label “progressivism.”
1. From cyclical history to linear progressBefore the Enlightenment, most European thinkers read the past as a cycle of rise‑and‑fall (classical “golden ages”) or a story of decline from a lost perfection (Augustinian theology). Eighteenth‑century philosophes overturned that view by arguing that reason and science can produce cumulative, irreversible gains in knowledge, technology and morality. Once you accept that axiom, “society should get better over time”—progress moves from possibility to duty. Stanford Encyclopedia of PhilosophyStanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

Up‑shot: By 1800 the leading current of European thought treated progress as law‑like, not accidental—laying the conceptual bedrock for any politics that takes reform as default.

3. Early reform projects—practice catches up to theoryEnlightenment monarchs and activists experimented with abolishing serfdom, expanding primary education, codifying legal equality, and curbing church privileges. Even critics like Rousseau, who worried about moral decay, judged societies by whether they could be made more just—another tribute to the new yard‑stick. Encyclopedia Britannica

4. Nineteenth‑century hand‑offThe idea outlasted the Enlightenment itself:

• Positivists (Saint‑Simon, Comte) argued that sociological “laws” could engineer social progress.
• Utilitarians (Bentham, Mill) reframed policy as a calculus for maximising aggregate well‑being.
• Abolitionists, suffragists, labour reformers adopted the rhetoric of moral and material advance to justify agitation.

By the 1880‑1910 window, English‑speakers simply started calling such reformers “progressives.” In the U.S. the term became a banner for trust‑busting, child‑labour laws, food‑safety regulation and women’s suffrage during the Progressive Era (1890s‑1920s). Wikipedia

5. Continuities & tensions

• Continuities: faith in empiricism, moral universalism, and the state as a legitimate tool for collective betterment.
• Tensions inherited from the Enlightenment:
• Technocratic optimism (Bacon, Condorcet) vs. romantic/communitarian critiques (Rousseau, later Marx).
• Universal reason vs. the exclusion of women, colonised peoples and racial minorities from the “universal.”
• Confidence in linear progress vs. 20th‑century catastrophes (wars, ecological crises) that called the arc into question
Yet even today, mainstream self‑described progressives still argue in Enlightenment grammar: deploy data, appeal to universal rights, and present policy as the next rung on humanity’s upward ladder. Dictionaries that link “progress” and “progressivism” are paying homage to a 250‑year‑old conversation. Wikipedia
