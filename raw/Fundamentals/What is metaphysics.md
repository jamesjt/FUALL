# What is metaphysics

What is metaphysics:
https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/metaphysics/

Okay, now that I'm less fuzzy:
Metaphysics usually gets broadly described as the study of reality (as opposed to the study of individual real entities). I see that mediaisdelicious already linked the SEP, so I won't do that again.To give you an example of what that can encompass, the metaphysics course at my school covers:
the nature of truth
the question of free will (i.e. whether and how we have it)
Ontology (what sorts of things exist, or how we carve up reality into distinctions like universals and particulars, as well as whether or not abstract entities such as numbers, relations between things, properties like squareness, etc... actually exist and how)
mereology (questions about what constitutes an object, and whether and object's identity is preserved despite a change in individual parts)
the nature of time (...don't ask, please, I do NOT need that headache again)
modality (how we can reason about claims like "it is possible that it will rain tomorrow" or "God necessarily does not exist," as well as what those sorts of claims entail about reality)
the hard problem of consciousness and the relationship between mind and body
To sum that up: I don't have a precise definition of metaphysics for you, I just have a variety of cases in which it is appropriate to say of them "this is metaphysics." A really simplified definition might be "those questions and discussions that are obviously philosophical in nature, but do not clearly fall under epistemology or ethics," but I'm sure someone more qualified than me will take issue with that.
To answer your second question: What do you consider progress or significant truth? There are some truths that are generally accepted by philosophers but that don't have much effect outside the discipline, and there are other philosophical ideas that affect the practice of other disciplines. Further, a good number of truths in philosophy are negative claims, e.g. "this doesn't work." These claims are a form of progress (we've ruled out a possibility), and further are consistent with a scientific view of progress (the rejection of the heliocentric view is generally viewed as progress, despite Copernicus' model being fairly inaccurate, ideologically driven, and not consistent with the observed facts at the time). Elimination of a possibility generally doesn't seem like philosophical progress, however, so that sort of thing can seem a bit cheap.
Anyways, some examples that immediately spring to mind:
Frege's investigation into the relationship between maths and logic (particularly applicable to computer science)
Kuhn's theory-ladenness of observation (even if Gadamer kinda beat him to the punch on it)
The rejection of logical positivism as untenable
Kripke's notation for modal logic as a means of reasoning about possibility and necessity
Russell's early work in hierarchical set theory
Austin's development of speech-act theory
Grice's idea of the cooperative principle (Linguists find these two extraordinarily helpful)
Gettier cases as a defeater to the Justified True Belief theory of knowledge
These are the ones I can think of off the top of my head. I'd say more, but I'm about to be late for work.
