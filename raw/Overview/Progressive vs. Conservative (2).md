# Progressive vs. Conservative (2)

The “progressive–conservative” spectrum is itself an aggregate measure: it compresses many separate issues—and the degree of desired change on each, and how to enact that change—into a single label. A person may be progressive on one topic yet conservative on another, and two people can be progressive on the same issue but disagree on what change to enact to solve the problem. Without incredibly clear context, blanket statements about where someone “sits” on the spectrum do not get sufficient meaning out of the statement.
Because the default stance in most systems is to preserve current practice, progressives must specify which practices they want to change and how. Broad, undefined “progressivism” tends to splinter quickly:
- Two people who agree on a problem may still disagree on the solution or the strategy for implementing it.
- The label “moderate” or “centrist” conveys little: it might describe someone extremely dissatisfied with a few issues and satisfied with others, or simply mildly satisfied across the board
- Vague, catch-all movements make it harder both to rally like-minded reformers and to persuade conservatives that a concrete, well-reasoned opportunity for improvement exists.
In short, effective progressive politics need targeted, clearly articulated goals; otherwise, the movement dissolves into incompatible sub-factions and loses the trust of potential allies—conservative and progressive alike.
