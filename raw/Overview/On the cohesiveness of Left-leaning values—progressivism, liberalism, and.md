On the cohesiveness of Left-leaning values—progressivism, liberalism, and collectivism

One persistent obstacle to progress is cost: opportunity cost, displacement cost, and exploration cost. The link between progressivism and collectivism lies largely in how society handles those displacement and exploration costs.
- Exploration costs. Ambitious projects such as the International Space Station, LIGO, and other large-scale scientific endeavors require resources far beyond what private benefactors can reliably supply. Because we all pay taxes, we can pool those resources and fund reality-exploring efforts—conceptually and literally—rather than relying on a handful of ultra-rich patrons to push science forward. Yet federal research budgets are being cut even as the need for collective investment grows.
- Displacement costs. Progress often displaces workers. Consider driverless cars and trucks: automation would be a huge boon for society, but its cost will fall on truckers who lose their livelihoods. Collectively, we should shoulder those costs rather than leaving displaced workers behind.
- Opportunity costs and liberalism. We are notoriously bad at predicting who will generate the next breakthrough—sorting for potential, wisdom, or good ideas at scale is extremely difficult and heavily influenced by luck. Expanding individual liberties gives more people room to explore, experiment, and innovate. Thus, liberalism and progressivism reinforce each other: the greater the liberty individuals possess, the more opportunities exist for genuine progress.
In short, progressivism urges us to move society forward, collectivism asks us to share the exploration and displacement costs that progress entails, and liberalism expands the pool of potential innovators whose lucky insights may drive the next big leap.
