# Terms for political movements

We need a more sophisticated way to discuss politics. A political movement is a set of issues behind which people concentrate power, so its name must clearly and intuitively signal what it seeks to change, conserve, or achieve.
A movement should at least identify a problem, the change it hopes to enact, or the element it wants to preserve—and, ideally, outline a plan for doing so. Sometimes that plan is missing because no one has yet investigated the root of the problem; the first step, then, is to secure resources to study the issue and design the most effective strategy. In worse circumstances, the harm is already great—often small harms at vast scale that “make us all dumber.” How long do we wait for perfect knowledge before taking our first stabs at becoming less dumb?
Every movement must define its goals, name itself accordingly, and select (or anoint) principal leaders. Those leaders might found the movement or arise later, but the brand-bearers must embody its values. Members must hold leaders accountable to those principles, recognizing that both values and strategy can evolve and that leaders must communicate such changes.
When followers identify with a group yet do not understand—or practice—the movement’s values, allegiance drifts from principle to personality. The result is no longer a political movement but a political cult: people follow power irrationally. Members must retain the power to remove, challenge, or criticize leaders who stray from the movement’s purpose, even though this can create endless factional splits. This is one of the hardest tasks—but we must manage it, or we’re just fucked.
In summary:
Labeling a cause merely “Left” or “Right” is useless for defining a movement, but discussing the underlying values is not.

Dig deeper: articulate a value structure, a goal, and (preferably) a strategy; appoint leaders committed to those values; and replace them if they betray the mission—even if doing so seems to weaken the movement in the short run. Better to correct a mistake than let an unprincipled leader deform the cause.

We saw this danger with BLM, with portions of the progressive movement, and with whatever “Left” and “Right” now mean.

Donald Trump at least modeled one constructive step: MAGA (and later MAHA) framed a problem and coined a label. The next step would be to spell out detailed value structures and metrics so we can measure efficacy, hold leaders to principle, and debate whether a given strategy is working.
That is where we must go in understanding—and renovating—the Left-Right conversation, rather than discarding the idea because it feels inadequate today.
