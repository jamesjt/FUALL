# Context

We often speak of a single political spectrum, Left vs Right.

Various writings are calling it useless, meaningless, nonsense, and it is a term that is confusing various people. 

Bret getting it right?
However, the real problem with it is that Left and Right are an aggregate of aggregates. 

And the thing that is is aggregating has been difficult to define in an easily transmissible way. 

This is in part because terms that represent important concepts are being abused by people seeking power (Politicians) degrading their utility making it more difficult for those aiming to make us wiser and build a better way of life (Statesman) to communicate clearly with the public. This seems to specifically and actively be done to blur that line between Politician and Statesman. 

Our first problem is that we use the same term to describe several related—but meaningfully different—things. That ambiguity is exploited by power-seekers (politicians) and makes it harder for true governors (statesmen) to communicate clearly.
A single term, like 'The Left' or 'Democrat' often conflates at least five concepts:

• Values (ideology)
• Party Brand (current public perception)
• Party Base (current beliefs of the social group claiming the term)
• Leaders’ speech (what party or movement leaders say)
• Leaders’ actions (what those leaders actually do)

I think what we neglect and what we need to best define are the terms related to the values, and what is what I'll be speaking to. For it is the values that we should be holding our parties, movements, and leaders accountable to.
The Second, even at the level of values, these labels are densely packed with meaning. “Left vs. Right” is really a shorthand that compresses three top-level spectra—each of which across multiple dimensions event further.
Although the terms arose from arbitrary seating arrangements, they have since accumulated substantial significance.

LeftRightProgressive        ConservativeLiberalAuthoritarianCollectivistIndividualist
Each cluster of ideas has internal coherence, yet society often stumbles when we start mixing and matching them across the underlying spectra.
Because the labels are intuitive, it is possible—and useful—to map them onto formal spectra, each with four analytical facets:

 • Telos – its purpose or utility 
 • Tensions – conflicts with the opposing end or with other spectra.
 • Trials – practical challenges it must overcome.
 • Tragedies – major pitfalls it must avoid.
