# Non-perfect Accuracy of Myths, Abstraction but still valuable

One theme that recurs throughout history—yet seems increasingly forgotten—is our drive to grasp what is true. We keep trying to understand aspects of reality, much as Plato described in his realm-of-Forms: at each moment in time we offer an approximation of that deeper truth.
Mythology illustrates this impulse. Myths are truth-seeking approximations: early observers saw the ocean behaving as if alive and reasoned that anything appearing animate must possess a spirit. They named that spirit Poseidon and eventually personified it as a god. In hindsight, we know the sea is not self-animated; instead, the Moon’s gravitational pull and other forces create tides and the complex motions we interpret as waves. The myth was clever—just data-poor.
Our political language follows a similar pattern. Today many dismiss “Left” versus “Right” as meaningless, yet their aim is merely misaligned. They overlook the complex forces acting on what those labels try to capture. Declaring the Left-Right distinction bogus is like calling the idea of Poseidon—or mythology itself—bogus. Mythology was not meaningless; it was an earnest attempt to explain the unknown. Likewise, Left versus Right once served as a rough explanatory model, but it is now overloaded with meaning that needs unpacking.
In truth, liberal versus conservative is no more the single, animating force of politics than Poseidon is the animating force of waves. We need more than one spectrum. By examining the rich content packed into Left and Right—just as astronomers examined lunar tides—we can refine, adjust, and improve our understanding.

Although the Left-Right axis arose from something arbitrary, conflicts over power keep recurring along lines that resemble it. Therefore, even terms as saturated with meaning as Left and Right merit close study rather than dismissal.
