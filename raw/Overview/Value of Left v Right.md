# Value of Left v Right

Value of "Left v Right"
One of the problems we face today concerns the political spectrum in its most abstract form: Left versus Right. We can introduce many other terms, yet even this basic formulation retains utility despite its arbitrary origin.

Much like units of measurement—ruler-length inches or centimeters—Left and Right began as arbitrary markers (people literally sat on opposite sides of a room), but the values now attached to them have become consistent and, to some degree, measurable. I am not claiming to have completed the work required to measure them fully, nor do I believe that the three spectra I am about to describe (with their sub-spectra) are definitively the right number.

We have arrived at a point where many people treat “leftist,” “progressive,” and “liberal” as synonyms. Although some speakers do use the words interchangeably, we should not do so conceptually. Others argue that the distinction between Left and Right—and the terms that fall under those umbrellas—is meaningless. This view appears everywhere, from casual internet posts to political-science articles and even among some political scientists.

Something important is missing. Because our cultural referees—the people we rely on to make sense of the world—are confused, they no longer know what “Left” means. Hence we hear claims such as “liberalism isn’t liberal” and similar complaints.

It is crucial that we develop language that clarifies our value structures, the purposes behind them, and the animating tensions that shape how we structure society along the Left–Right axis.
