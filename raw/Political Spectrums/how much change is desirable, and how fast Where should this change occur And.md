# how much change is desirable, and how fast Where should this change occur And

how much change is desirable, and how fast? Where should this change occur? And what is our strategy for accomplishing it? 

Great questions—this pushes us into the heart of strategic political philosophy: how much change is desirable, and how fast? And more crucially: how should a society’s current state—thriving, stable, or declining—affect its risk appetite?

Let’s walk through this.

🔢 What the Numbers Mean to Me
This is a risk-orientation spectrum, not a partisan or policy axis.
ValueMentalityMotto0–10Revolutionary“Burn it down—almost anything is better.”20–30Visionary-radical“We need systemic transformation, fast.”35–45Measured-progressive“Let’s evolve—carefully.”50–65Conservative-institutional“Fix small things, protect what works.”70–90Reactionary“Return to what once was.”90–100Dogmatic-preservationist“Stop the change, at all costs.”
So, 40 implies a society that:
- Is forward-looking, but
- Anchors that change in risk-awareness, incrementalism, and feedback loops.
It’s ideal when the society’s systems are mostly functional but imperfect—like a complex machine that needs upgrades, not demolition.
❓ Why not ~25?25 implies a much higher tolerance for systemic risk—a willingness to undo foundational aspects of the current order, not just reform them.
That may be justified when:
- The present system is not just flawed but failing
- Evolution is too slow to respond to urgent threats
- The status quo causes active harm at scale
But here’s the trade-off:
- Societies at 25 can improve faster, but also collapse faster.
- The chance of nonlinear catastrophe increases.
- Moral urgency rises, but institutional memory weakens.
In short: ~25 is a revolution with a conscience. ~40 is a reformation with a blueprint.

🏚️ But what about decline? Shouldn't we go lower?Yes and no—depends on the kind of decline and the quality of alternatives.If the U.S. is:
- Materially declining (infrastructure, life expectancy, civic trust),
- Culturally fragmented, and
- Politically paralyzed…
Then yes: a higher appetite for risk is justified.
BUT—risk appetite isn’t the same as clarity of direction.

A declining society with no consensus on what counts as improvement→ tends to swing wildly between radical impulses and conservative backlash
→ e.g., Weimar Germany, late Qing China, 1990s Russia
In other words: Decline increases pressure for transformation, but it also increases the cost of getting it wrong.
So I might shift the ideal society from ~40 to ~30 in a decline scenario—but only if:
- There’s visionary clarity about ends
- There’s institutional capacity to absorb disruption
- The public has some moral consensus
Otherwise, bold reform becomes chaos marketing.

🧠 What would change my mindI’d support dropping to ~25 or below only if:
- Existing systems are actively harmful and irreformable
- New systems are coherently proposed, broadly supported, and tested at scale
- There's a leadership class that combines moral seriousness with practical wisdom
This is rare.

🏁 Final Thought“If your house is on fire, you don’t worry about the blueprints—you just get out. But if it’s got termites, you want to renovate carefully, not bulldoze it at midnight.”
So yes—America’s decline justifies more risk, but not blind risk. I still favor a position around ~35–38: reformist, impatient, but deliberate. Radicalism without a moral and architectural north star just accelerates decay.
